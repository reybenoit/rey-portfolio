The Problem

"A lyricist who couldn't hear his own songs."

Most music tools are built for people who already know production. I'd been writing lyrics for years before I could put words to what was wrong.

The problem isn't creativity. It isn't access to tools. The problem is the gap between having a song in your head and being able to hear it back — and nobody had built a direct path across it. HEARD is that path.

The Insight

The barrier to music creation isn't creativity. It's production literacy.

Before writing a line of code, I ran an AI-assisted self-interview to pressure-test the problem and find its real edges. The process surfaced something I'd felt for years but hadn't named: writers hit a wall the moment their idea needs sound.

Not because they lack talent — because music production is a separate discipline that takes years to learn. HEARD is built on that insight. The question isn't "can you write?" It's "can you hear what you've written?"

The Decision

Fidelity vs. Velocity

What got cut
Voice Cloning

Generating songs in an artist's own voice was technically within reach and emotionally compelling — the kind of feature that makes a demo unforgettable. It was also a 6-month engineering detour with unresolved legal exposure. Cutting it wasn't a compromise. It was a decision.

What shipped
Text to Music

A generator focused on the core use case: give me a song from a lyric or concept, fast. No production knowledge required. One clear problem, solved well. Real users, real output, real value — without waiting for a feature that could wait.

Scope control is a design skill.

Outcome
Weeks → Minutes

Production time collapsed from weeks of back-and-forth to minutes per track.

Deployed and accessible. Real users, real feedback, real iteration.

About

I started in agency work — at DigitasLBi, designing for brands like Taco Bell, Warner Music Group, and American Express. That world taught me craft and speed: how to make something real, fast, and with enough wit to cut through. The Taco Bell mobile app we built launched a category. 1.4 million downloads. A Webby. The discipline of moving fast without losing taste became the foundation for everything after.

Seven years at Google Cloud changed what design meant to me. When you're building for enterprise, the interface is only part of the job. The harder problem is organizational — getting design into decisions made two levels above the product. I grew from IC to manager, eventually leading UX across a portfolio responsible for a significant share of the org's revenue. The work stopped being about screens and started being about systems: process, prioritization, how a team creates the conditions for the right things to get built.

Most recently, I built HEARD — an AI music generator for lyricists who can write songs but can't produce them. I built it because I was that lyricist. Designed the experience, coded the stack, shipped it solo from zero to a live product with real users. The through-line isn't the technology. It's the gap: who gets left out, and how do we close that distance?

What connects all of it: access, craft, and genuine care for the people I work with. I'm most alive when the technology is new, the team is curious, and the work expands what's possible.

How I work

01
Designing for Scale

The hardest design problems aren't the ones in front of users. They're the ones inside the org — duplicated work, conflicting patterns, decisions made without design at the table.

At Google Cloud, I managed design across 4 global locations, supporting 10 PMs and 12 engineers. I built the systems that let designers focus on problems instead of process — templates, workflows, design studios, office hours, a quantitative UI prioritization model.

I mandated that AlloyDB and Cloud SQL be designed concurrently wherever possible. That single call eliminated redundant work across 10+ features and accelerated time-to-market for GCBDR, Final Backups, PITR, and more. Good systems design is invisible — you see it in what doesn't break.

02
Shipping

I believe in moving. Not rushing — moving. There's a difference between velocity that creates debt and velocity that creates momentum.

I've shipped a new database product in 9 months. I've turned around a Cloud Next launch in two weeks. I've built and deployed HEARD in the time most teams spend in kickoff.

None of those happened by accident. They happened because scope was controlled, decisions were made early, and the team knew what done looked like.

03
Building the Team

The best thing I've ever shipped is a designer who didn't think they were ready.

I've promoted two L3s to L4 and one L4 to L5. I've built a vibe coding toolkit with one designer, powered a UX workflow with another, and earned a top-3 finish in a 36-team ideathon with a third. I run bi-weekly team meetings that open with icebreakers because I believe you can't do hard work with people you don't know.

My manager survey says "good across all categories." What it can't quantify is the culture — and that's the part I'm most proud of.

Why I design
Design is the practice of closing distance.

The distance between what a technology promises and what a person can actually do with it. I've worked at that gap in music, in enterprise software, and in AI for 17 years — not because it's an interesting problem, but because I believe the tools we build either expand what people can do or quietly exclude them.

Every project starts with the same question: who's being left out? The work is trying to close that distance, one decision at a time. That conviction has followed me from consumer apps to enterprise infrastructure to AI products. The domain changes. The question stays the same.

I lead with that question. And I build teams that ask it too.

Let's talk

Good work starts with a conversation.

I'm open to senior design and design leadership roles — particularly in AI, consumer products, and creative tools. If the work is new and the team is curious, I'd love to hear from you.
